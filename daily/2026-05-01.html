Daily Macro Brief — 2026-05-01

_As of: Fri May 1, 2026 (US close / evening ET)._

Executive Take

Markets are sending a “mixed risk” signal: US equities were uneven (tech/NDQ stronger, Dow weaker), while oil fell sharply, gold rose, and the dollar firmed slightly. Meanwhile, the data side did *not* look like a clean disinflation story: ISM Manufacturing stayed in expansion (PMI 52.7) but the Prices index jumped to 84.6 (highest since Apr 2022) while employment remained in contraction (46.4).

Overlay that with the Fed’s late-April message — rates held at 3.50%–3.75%, inflation “elevated” partly due to global energy prices, and high uncertainty tied to the Middle East — and the base case remains: policy is biased toward “wait + data,” with inflation sensitivity high.

Macro / Policy: What Happened

Fed (Apr 29 FOMC statement)

  • Held the target range at 3.50%–3.75%.
  • Noted inflation is elevated, “in part reflecting the recent increase in global energy prices.”
  • Highlighted Middle East uncertainty.
  • Voting details signal internal tension: one dissenter preferred a 25 bp cut, while several supported holding rates but objected to an easing bias in the statement language.

Implementation details (Apr 29): IORB 3.65%, ON RRP offered at 3.50% with $160B/day per-counterparty limit.

ISM Manufacturing (Apr 2026)

  • PMI: 52.7 (unchanged vs March) — 4th straight month in expansion.
  • Prices: 84.6 (up 6.3 pts m/m) — highest since April 2022.
  • Employment: 46.4 (down 2.3 pts m/m) — still contracting.

Why It Matters

  • The inflation impulse is not gone if producer/survey prices are re-accelerating. Even with oil down on the day, ISM’s price pressures suggest pipeline inflation risk (and margin pressure) remains.
  • Growth is “okay-ish” but not clean: PMIs above 50 point to expansion, but employment subcomponents contracting is consistent with firms protecting margins / leaning on productivity rather than hiring.
  • Policy risk stays two-sided: the Fed explicitly flagged uncertainty and energy-driven inflation. That increases the probability that market “good news” (e.g., oil down) may not translate into near-term easing if price pressures stay hot.

Strongest counter-interpretation: ISM Prices can be noisy and may be capturing a temporary energy/war shock rather than broad-based inflation. If oil continues to fall and labor data softens, the next prints (CPI/PPI/jobs) could re-anchor expectations toward disinflation.
